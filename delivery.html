How Does Gene Therapy Work?

Gene delivery systems are critical components of gene therapy, responsible for transporting genetic material into target cells. These systems can be broadly categorized into viral and non-viral vectors, each with its advantages and challenges.

See Details: https://patienteducation.asgct.org/gene-therapy-101/vectors-101

The different types of Gene Delivery Systems:

Viral Vectors

  • 1. Adenoviruses

    Advantages: High transduction efficiency, can infect both dividing and non-dividing cells, large genetic payload capacity.

    Challenges: Can cause strong immune responses, transient expression of the therapeutic gene.


  • 2. Adeno-Associated Viruses (AAV)

    Advantages: Low immunogenicity, ability to infect a variety of cell types, stable long-term expression.

    Challenges: Limited genetic payload capacity (around 4.7 kb), production and scalability issues.


  • 3. Retroviruses

    Advantages: Can transduce both dividing and non-dividing cells, integration into the host genome for long-term expression, relatively high payload capacity.

    Challenges: Risk of insertional mutagenesis, complex production process.


  • 4. Lentiviruses

    Advantages: Integrate into the host genome, providing long-term expression; efficient transduction of dividing cells.

    Challenges: Risk of insertional mutagenesis, can only infect dividing cells, relatively lower payload capacity.





Non- Viral Vectors

  • 1. Liposomes

    Advantages: Low immunogenicity, can carry large genetic payloads, relatively easy and scalable production.

    Challenges: Low transfection efficiency compared to viral vectors, transient expression, potential cytotoxicity.


  • 2. Polymeric Nanoparticles

    Advantages: Versatile and customizable, can protect genetic material from degradation, potential for targeted delivery.

    Challenges: Variable transfection efficiency, potential for cytotoxicity, complex formulation.


  • 3. Electroporation

    Advantages: High transfection efficiency, suitable for ex vivo gene therapy.

    Challenges: Can cause cell damage, less suitable for in vivo applications, requires specialized equipment.


  • 4. Gene Gun (Biolistics)

    Advantages: Direct delivery to tissues, can be used for DNA vaccines.

    Challenges: Limited to superficial tissues, potential for tissue damage, variable transfection efficiency.


  • 5. Peptide-Based Delivery

    Advantages: Low toxicity, can facilitate targeted delivery, relatively easy to produce.

    Challenges: Limited transfection efficiency, stability issues.

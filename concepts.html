Concepts Of Genetics

Gregor Mendel


Father of Genetics Gregor Mendel, through his work on pea plants, discovered the fundamental laws of inheritance. He deduced that genes come in pairs and are inherited as distinct units, one from each parent. Mendel tracked the segregation of parental genes and their appearance in the offspring as dominant or recessive traits. He recognized the mathematical patterns of inheritance from one generation to the next. Mendel's Laws of Heredity are usually stated as:

  • 1) The Law of Segregation: Each inherited trait is defined by a gene pair. Parental genes are randomly separated to the sex cells so that sex cells contain only one gene of the pair. Offspring therefore inherit one genetic allele from each parent when sex cells unite in fertilization.
  • 2) The Law of Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits are sorted separately from one another so that the inheritance of one trait is not dependent on the inheritance of another.
  • 3) The Law of Dominance: An organism with alternate forms of a gene will express the form that is dominant.

The genetic experiments Mendel did with pea plants took him eight years (1856-1863) and he published his results in 1865. During this time, Mendel grew over 10,000 pea plants, keeping track of progeny number and type. Mendel's work and his Laws of Inheritance were not appreciated in his time.

He demonstrated that traits are transmitted faithfully from parents to offspring independently of other traits and in dominant and recessive patterns. It wasn't until 1900, after the rediscovery of his Laws, that his experimental results were understood.

How Do Genetic Concepts Shape the Development and Application of Gene Therapy?


In the world of biology, genetics stands as a fundamental cornerstone, deciphering the complex mechanisms governing the transmission and diversity of traits among living beings. From Gregor Mendel's groundbreaking work with pea plants to the cutting-edge innovations of contemporary gene therapy, this discipline has undergone remarkable progress, fundamentally shaping our comprehension of life's intricacies.

Let's start with Mendelian Genetics. In the mid-19th century, Gregor Mendel meticulously studied the patterns of inheritance in pea plants, laying down the fundamental principles that govern how traits are passed from parents to offspring. His laws of segregation and independent assortment provided a framework for understanding heredity that still holds true today.

As science progressed, Molecular Genetics emerged as a key player, delving into the molecular structure and function of genes. With the discovery of DNA as the genetic material and the elucidation of its structure by Watson and Crick, scientists gained unprecedented insight into the mechanisms of heredity. Molecular genetics explores how genes are transcribed into RNA and translated into proteins, as well as the intricate regulatory networks that control gene expression.

Population Genetics takes a broader perspective, examining the genetic variation within and between populations of organisms. By studying factors such as gene flow, genetic drift, natural selection, and mutation, population geneticists gain insights into the evolutionary processes that shape biodiversity.

Gene therapy, drawing on genetic principles, employs targeted interventions utilizing genetics research to identify disease-causing genes, molecular genetics techniques like CRISPR-Cas9 for precise gene manipulation, and insights from population genetics to assess its impact across diverse populations.

Genetics serves as the bedrock upon which gene therapy is built. By leveraging insights from Mendelian genetics, molecular genetics, and population genetics, scientists are paving the way for a new era of personalized medicine, where genetic diseases can be treated at their root cause.

History of Gene Therapy

1970

Stanfield Roger proposes the concept of gene therapy, suggesting that healthy DNA could replace faulty DNA in individuals with genetic disorders.

1972

Theodore Friedmann and Richard Roblin publish the paper "Gene therapy for human genetic disease?" in Science, further exploring the potential of gene therapy.

1985

Dr. W. French Anderson and Michael Blease begin their pioneering work on gene therapy, demonstrating how cells from individuals with adenosine deaminase (ADA) deficiency can be altered in culture.

1986

Initial attempts are made to transfer correct genes into the bone marrow of animals as part of gene therapy research.

1988

Anderson and Blease achieve significant success in transferring correct genes into white blood cells, marking a significant advancement in gene therapy techniques.

1989

Anderson, Blease, and Dr. Steven Rosenberg collaborate to investigate the safety and effectiveness of gene therapy in cancer patients, using modified tumor infiltration lymphocytes (TIL cells) from individuals with malignant melanoma.

1990

The first documented case of gene therapy occurs when a four-year-old girl with ADA deficiency is treated at the NIH Clinical Center. Her white blood cells are extracted, genetically modified to produce ADA, and reintroduced into her system.

1993

Gene therapy is administered to newborn infants with ADA deficiency, marking further expansion of the approach to younger patients. Corrected ADA genes are transferred into immature blood cells obtained from umbilical cords.
